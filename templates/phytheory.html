Phase Difference as a Function of Time Difference

📌 Introduction

Imagine a hanging bell gently swinging back and forth — that’s our Simple Pendulum. In this virtual lab, we will understand the phase difference between two points during its swing. Sounds easy? Let’s dive in!

📎 Key Definitions

  • 🎯 Simple Pendulum: A simple pendulum is a mass (bob) suspended by a light, inextensible string that swings back and forth under the force of gravity.
  • ⏱️ Time Period: Time taken for one complete swing (forward and back).
  • 📐 Phase: Phase refers to the position of a point within a wave cycle of a light wave, usually measured in degrees or radians.
  • 🔄 Oscillation: Oscillation is the repetitive back-and-forth movement around the equilibrium position.
  • 📊 Amplitude: The maximum distance the pendulum moves from its central (rest) position.
  • ⚖️ Equlibrium Position:Central position where the object naturally settles when no external forces are applied.
  • 🎵 Frequency: The number of oscillations completed in one second. Formula: f = 1 / T
  • ↔ Displacement: The distance of the pendulum bob from the equlibrium position at any point in time.

📍 Phase Difference Between Two Points

The phase difference between two points in a wave refers to the difference in the phase of the wave at those two points, typically measured in radians or degrees.

Δφ = (2π / T) × Δt

Where:
Δφ = Phase difference (in radians and degree )
T = Time period of the pendulum
Δt = Time difference between the two positions

Imagine your friend said “hi” at one end of the pendulum and another replied “hello” a bit later at the other end — the time between them is your Δt!

🧠 Mathematical Expression

General equation for Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) of pendulum:

x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)

Where:
x(t) = Position at time t
A = Amplitude (maximum swing)
ω = Angular frequency (ω = 2π / T)
φ = Phase constant (starting position of pendulum)

🆚 Simple vs Compound Pendulum

🎯 Simple Pendulum

A small mass (bob) suspended by a light, inextensible string swinging under gravity. Assumes all mass is concentrated at a single point.

T = 2π √(L / g)

T is the period of the pendulum in seconds (s).
L is the length of the pendulum in (m).
g is the acceleration due to gravity in (m/s²)

🏗️ Compound Pendulum

A compound pendulum is a rigid body suspended from a fixed horizontal axis and its time period depends on its moment of inertia and the distance from the pivot to the center of mass.

T = 2π √(I / mgh)

I = Moment of inertia in kg·m²
m = Mass in kg
g = Gravity in m/s²
h = Distance to center of mass in meters(m)
Think of the simple pendulum as a playground swing and the compound one as a heavy swinging door. Both move rhythmically, but the physics behind them differs!

🎯 Conditions of the Experiment

  • The angle should be small (≤15°) — otherwise, it won’t follow SHM properly.
  • The string should be thin and tight — not stretchy like a rubber band.
  • The bob (mass) should be small and solid.
  • The pivot point should be frictionless — or else, the pendulum will stop too soon.
